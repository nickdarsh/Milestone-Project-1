HTTP 1.1 and 2.0: The Protocols of Efficiency and Speed

The Internet Boom and HTTP 1.1 (1997)

The mid to late 1990s witnessed an explosive growth of the commercial internet, also known as the dot-com boom, a period of innovation and investment. The release of HTTP 1.1 in 1997 marked a significant milestone, focusing on reliability and efficiency to support the world of e-commerce and dynamic web applications.

The most important concept introduced in 1.1 was persistent connections, which allowed multiple requests and responses through a single TCP connection. This greatly improved performance and reduced latency.

The Modern Web and HTTP/2 (2015)

By the late 2000s, the web had evolved into a complex ecosystem with high-definition media and data-intensive services. The limitations of HTTP 1.1, especially the issue where a single slow request could hold up subsequent requests (**Head-of-Line Blocking**), became apparent.

This bottleneck inspired the development of a new version, heavily influenced by Google’s SPDY protocol. In 2015, the IETF published HTTP/2, which included a transformative feature:

  • Multiplexing: This allowed for multiple requests and responses to be sent in parallel over a single connection, fundamentally solving the latency and performance issues of its predecessor.

HTTP/2 laid the groundwork for the modern, fast, and data-rich internet experience.
